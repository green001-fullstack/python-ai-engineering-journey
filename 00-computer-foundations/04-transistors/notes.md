A transistor is, at its heart, an electronic switch.

Instead of your finger moving the switch...

Electricity controls it.

Think of it like this.

Your Finger
      │
      ▼
Light Switch

ON / OFF

A transistor is more like this.

Electric Signal
       │
       ▼
Transistor

ON / OFF

That's it.

At least, that's the basic idea.

# Here's Something Mind-Blowing

Your laptop's processor may contain tens of billions of transistors.

Not millions. Billions.

Each one is switching on and off incredibly quickly.

Imagine trying to manually flip billions of light switches every second.

Impossible.

Yet that's essentially what's happening electronically inside the chip.

# Why Binary?

Now we can answer a question we touched on earlier.

Remember when we asked:

"Why do computers use 0 and 1?"

Many people say:

"Because computers like binary."

No.

Computers don't "like" anything.

The real reason is much simpler.

A switch naturally has two stable states.

ON

OFF

Those map beautifully to:

1

0

Nature gave us the hardware.

Humans built computation on top of it.

# Next big step
Now imagine we have several transistors.

Can we arrange them so that:

A light turns on only if two switches are on?
A signal passes through only if another signal is off?
We can choose between two paths?

The answer is yes.

And those arrangements are called...

Logic Gates

Logic gates are where electricity starts behaving like reasoning.

Not intelligence.

Reasoning.

They answer questions like:

AND
OR
NOT
